NIGERIA COURT HIERARCHY AND JUDICIAL SYSTEM
===========================================

OVERVIEW
--------
The Nigerian judicial system comprises different types of courts that exist in layers of superiority, each with specific functions. These courts form the judicial arm of government, ensuring a clear separation of powers as entrenched in the Nigerian constitution. The executive and legislative arms do not interfere in the activities of the Judiciary.

THE 8 HIERARCHY OF COURTS IN NIGERIA
====================================

1. SUPREME COURT OF NIGERIA
---------------------------
- Position: Highest court in the country
- Established: 1963
- Location: Supreme Court Building, Three Arms Zone, Abuja, FCT
- Composition: Chief Justice of Nigeria and up to 21 Justices
- Appointment: By the President upon recommendation of the National Judicial Council (NJC) and approval by the Senate
- Retirement Age: 70 years mandatory

Functions:
- Final court of appeal for all civil and criminal cases in Nigeria
- Settles disputes between states, and between state and federal government
- Interpreter of the law and constitution
- Guardian of the constitution
- Advisory role to the executive
- Original jurisdiction in disputes between states and between federal government and states

Current Chief Justice: Justice Kudirat Motonmori Olatokunbo Kekere-Ekun, GCON (appointed 22 August 2024)

2. COURT OF APPEAL
------------------
- Position: Intermediate appellate court of the Nigerian federal court system
- Locations: 72 appeal courts across different states
- Leadership: President of the Court of Appeal
- Composition: 66 judges approved by the Senate

Functions:
- Intermediate appellate court of the Nigerian federal court system
- Original jurisdiction to determine validity of elections for President, Vice President, Governor, or Deputy Governor
- Appeals from High Courts, Sharia Courts of Appeal, Customary Courts of Appeal, and National Industrial Court
- Review decisions of administrative bodies and tribunals

3. FEDERAL HIGH COURT
---------------------
- Position: Third in hierarchy
- Leadership: Chief Judge
- Jurisdiction: Primarily civil cases
- Locations: More than 15 states in the country

Functions:
- Court of first instance for federal matters
- Exclusive jurisdiction over taxation, banking, custom and excise duties, citizenship, copyright
- Admiralty cases and cases involving the federal government
- Civil cases as set out under Section 251(1) of the 1999 Constitution

4. STATE HIGH COURT
-------------------
- Constitutional Provision: Section 255 of the 1999 Constitution
- Leadership: Chief Judge (appointed by State House of Assembly, National Assembly for FCT)
- Jurisdiction: Largest jurisdiction under the 1999 Constitution

Functions:
- Authority to hear both criminal and civil cases in the state
- Appellate jurisdiction over decisions of Customary Courts, Area Courts, Magistrate Courts
- General jurisdiction at state level for civil and criminal matters within state jurisdiction

5. NATIONAL INDUSTRIAL COURT (NIC)
----------------------------------
- Established: 1976
- Leadership: President of the National Industrial Court
- Location: 11, New Bussa Close, Area 3, Garki, Abuja
- Powers: All powers of High Court of a state plus appellate jurisdiction

Functions:
- Adjudicate trade disputes and labour practices
- Matters related to Factories Act, Trade Disputes Act, Trade Unions Act, Workers' Compensation Law
- Disputes arising from employment relationships (wrongful dismissal, redundancy, discrimination)
- Exclusive jurisdiction over industrial disputes

6. SHARIA COURT OF APPEAL
-------------------------
- Leadership: Grand Kadi
- Composition: Number of Kadis
- Locations: Abuja and Northern states that practice Sharia law
- Jurisdiction: Appellate jurisdiction in civil cases involving Islamic personal law

Functions:
- Reviewing cases relating to Sharia and Islamic law
- Interpreting customary laws as bound by the constitution
- Handling litigation cases, particularly for the poor
- Civil cases involving issues of Islamic personal law

7. CUSTOMARY COURT OF APPEAL
----------------------------
- Leadership: President of the Customary Court of Appeal
- Composition: Judges
- Locations: FCT and states that establish them
- Jurisdiction: Supervisory and appellate jurisdiction in civil cases involving customary law

Functions:
- Intermediate appellate court for customary law cases
- Jurisdiction to hear appeals from customary courts
- Civil cases involving issues of customary law

8. MAGISTRATE COURTS AND DISTRICT COURTS
----------------------------------------
- Establishment: By State House of Assembly law
- Names: District Courts (Northern Nigeria), Magistrate Courts (Southern Nigeria)
- Function: Summary judgment judicial body
- Position: Lowest courts in the hierarchy

Functions:
- Handle cases at grassroots level
- Initial entry point for most legal matters in Nigeria
- Summary determination without briefs or pleadings
- Limited jurisdiction over criminal and civil matters
- Criminal cases: Minor offences
- Civil disputes: Monetary value up to N5 million

COURT JURISDICTION SUMMARY
=========================

FEDERAL COURTS:
- Supreme Court: Constitutional matters, final appeals
- Court of Appeal: Appeals from lower courts, election disputes
- Federal High Court: Federal revenue, banking, immigration, maritime law
- National Industrial Court: Labour and industrial disputes

STATE COURTS:
- State High Court: General civil and criminal jurisdiction
- Magistrate/District Courts: Minor offences, small civil disputes

SPECIALIZED COURTS:
- Sharia Court of Appeal: Islamic personal law
- Customary Court of Appeal: Traditional/customary law matters

APPOINTMENT PROCESS
==================
- Chief Justice: Nominated by President upon NJC recommendation, confirmed by Senate
- Supreme Court Justices: Appointed by President upon NJC recommendation, Senate approval
- Other Federal Judges: Similar process through NJC
- State Judges: State-level process with NJC involvement

JUDICIAL ADMINISTRATION
======================
- National Judicial Council (NJC): Recommends judicial appointments
- Federal Judicial Service Commission: Administrative support
- State Judicial Service Commissions: State-level administration

CONSTITUTIONAL FRAMEWORK
========================
- 1999 Constitution of the Federal Republic of Nigeria provides the legal framework
- Separation of powers ensures judicial independence
- Federal and state court systems operate within defined jurisdictions
- Appeals process ensures access to justice at multiple levels

This hierarchy ensures orderly administration of justice from grassroots level through the highest court, with clear jurisdictional boundaries and appeal processes for all legal matters in Nigeria.

RECENT LANDMARK SUPREME COURT CASES (2023-2024)
===============================================

1. NATIONAL INLAND WATERWAYS AUTHORITY v. LAGOS STATE (2024)
- Date: January 5, 2024
- Issue: Jurisdiction over waterways and maritime law
- Significance: Clarified federal vs state authority over inland waterways

2. FEDERAL GOVERNMENT v. 36 STATE GOVERNMENTS (2024)
- Date: July 11, 2024
- Issue: Local government autonomy and fund allocation
- Significance: Landmark decision on fiscal federalism and local government independence

3. LOTTERY REGULATION CASE (2024)
- Date: October 14, 2024
- Issue: Regulatory authority over lottery operations
- Significance: Redefined gambling and lottery regulation framework

4. RIVERS STATE FUND ALLOCATION CASE (2024)
- Issue: Blocked fund allocation due to political crisis
- Significance: Set precedent for federal intervention in state affairs

5. INHERITANCE RIGHTS CASES (2023-2024)
- Multiple cases addressing women's inheritance rights
- Significance: Progressive interpretation of customary law vs constitutional rights

CONSTITUTIONAL PROVISIONS AND RECENT INTERPRETATIONS
===================================================

Key Constitutional Articles Frequently Interpreted:
- Article 6: Judicial Powers
- Article 230-234: Supreme Court jurisdiction and composition
- Article 251: Federal High Court jurisdiction
- Article 4: Legislative powers and federal/state division
- Article 162-168: Revenue allocation and fiscal federalism

Recent Constitutional Interpretations:
1. Local Government Autonomy (2024): Supreme Court affirmed constitutional independence of local governments
2. Revenue Allocation: Ongoing cases on federal allocation formula
3. Electoral Disputes: Multiple cases on election petition procedures
4. Fundamental Rights: Progressive interpretation of gender equality and inheritance rights
